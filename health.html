SECTION 5: Health and Wellbeing Contexts

Presenting health and wellbeing contexts are parental mental illness, substance use, trauma, disability, grief, and environmental stress. The early childhood is the time of active growing of a child where the optimal influence is a stable and responsive caregiving. The Topics 3, 6 and 10 lecture material highlights the fact that wellbeing is a characteristic of the relationships, environments and social conditions around the children and not just resilient individuals.

Understanding the Context

The ecological systems theory by Bronfenbrenner describes how family issues in the micro system, including the mental illness or even trauma of the parents tend to directly influence the children with the disrupted patterns of care giving. The exosystem stressors, such as housing or lack of access to health services, further raise the risk. The Children, Families and Communities readings shine light on how depression, anxiety, and substance dependence of parents are likely to cause inconsistencies in routines, emotional availability, and impaired attachment.

These issues are supported by modern Australian studies. Kaur et al. (2022) established that parental mental illness exposes children to the risk of emotional and behavioural problems in childhood. McLean et al. (2023) showed that trauma affects the stress response of the children, their self-regulation, and the transition behaviours. According to Hodge et al. (2024), it was found that there are sustained health disparities within the communities of the regions and low-income communities, as families are not allowed as much access to early intervention and mental health assistance.

Disaster and COVID-19 pandemic readings at lectures also highlight that environmental stress influences children in relation to their sleeping, behaviour, communication, and emotional stability. The results of this study indicate that educators should be aware of extraneous stressors when explaining behaviours.

Impact on Children and Families

The problems of health and wellbeing are presented in the emotional development, social development, and cognitive development of children. Children who have undergone prolonged stress or trauma can either be withdrawn, hypervigilant or aggressive. According to the lecture materials of topic 3, stress has the potential to hinder executive functioning and emotional regulation, making the learning process more challenging. In cases where parents have their own mental health issues, routines and responsive care giving is difficult to maintain and this brings about insecurity in the children.

The disability, trauma, and mental health readings explain that children tend to take on the family stress as they become excessively responsible, uneasy or unsure when in new environments. The researchers discovered that children who have experienced trauma will need extra assurance and continuity to engage in a group setting with confidence (McLean et al., 2023). Social play and loneliness were also impacted by pandemic disruptions which impacted wellbeing.

Parents who struggle with mental illness, trauma or substance dependence might be guilty or afraid of being judged and thus find it difficult to seek help. Migrant and First Nation families can have other obstacles connected with stigma and cultural misunderstanding or other negative experiences of using services. This amplifies the importance of early childhood environments being protective, affordable, and non-judging environments to families.

Social Policy and Australian Responses

The Australian early childhood policy puts wellbeing as the basis of learning. The wellbeing is listed as one of the main Learning Outcomes in EYLF V2.0 and prompted by trauma-informed, holistic, and culturally responsive practice (AGDE, 2022). The National Quality Standard facilitates this as Quality Area 2 (health and safety) and Quality Area 5 (relationships).

The national and state plans such as the National Childrenâ€™s Mental Health and Wellbeing Strategy have emphasised on early intervention, coordinated support and cross-agency strategies. Nevertheless, Hodge et al. (2024) emphasize the existence of ongoing inequalities, especially among the regional and low-SES families. Disaster and pandemic readings indicate the lack of continuity of care in case of crisis events, and families lack consistent assistance.

Strategies for Practice

Effective strategies supported by research and policy include:

  1. Embedding trauma-informed practice, offering calm spaces, predictable routines, and sensitive responses.
  2. Strengthening co-regulation, helping children manage emotions through warm, attuned relationships.
  3. Using wellbeing-focused pedagogies, including mindfulness, sensory tools, and emotion coaching.
  4. Engaging families respectfully, using flexible communication and avoiding judgement.
  5. Identifying concerns early and making referrals, collaborating with health and mental health services.

These strategies align with Topics 6 and 10, which position educators as stabilising figures during family stress.

Community and Professional Partnerships

Close collaboration with child and family health nurses, mental health practitioners, alcohol and drug services, early intervention providers, Aboriginal community-controlled organisations, and parenting networks provide the families with the holistic support. Such collaborations can facilitate information exchange, multidisciplinary planning, and coordinated care, which are in line with EYLF V2.0 and national wellbeing approaches.

Resources for Educators and Children

Examples of supports are Be You, Headspace Early Childhood, Parentline and the online tools of the National Children Mental Health Strategy. The Huge Bag of Worries, The Rabbit Listened, When Sophie Gets Angry and A Terrible Thing Happened are books that promote the expression of emotions and reassurance. Media that is friendly to children such as Cosmic Kids Yoga, episodes of emotional literacy by Daniel Tiger, episodes of the Mindfully series in Play School, and Bedtime Explorers in ABC Kids radio assist children in developing coping strategies, as well as, in interpreting emotions in a nonjudgmental and gentle way.
